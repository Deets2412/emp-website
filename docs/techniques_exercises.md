# Practical Techniques & Exercises Guide
## Financial Psychology Course Implementation

### PURPOSE
This document provides specific, actionable techniques and exercises for each psychological modality as applied to financial behavior change. Each exercise is categorized by learning style to ensure accessibility.

---

## SECTION 1: CBT TECHNIQUES FOR MONEY

### 1.1 Money Thought Record

**Learning Styles**: Reading/Writing, Visual
**Duration**: 15 minutes daily

**Instructions**:
1. Identify triggering financial situation
2. Record automatic thoughts
3. Note emotions and intensity (0-100)
4. Identify cognitive distortions
5. Challenge with evidence
6. Create balanced thought
7. Re-rate emotion intensity

**Template**:
| Situation | Automatic Thought | Emotion (0-100) | Distortion | Evidence For/Against | Balanced Thought | New Emotion |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|------------|---------------------|------------------|-------------|
| Saw credit card bill | I'm drowning in debt, I'll never recover | Anxiety (90) | Catastrophizing | For: Bill is high. Against: I've paid before, have job, can make plan | This is challenging but manageable with a plan | Anxiety (50) |

### 1.2 Financial Fear Hierarchy (Exposure Therapy)

**Learning Styles**: Visual, Kinesthetic
**Duration**: Ongoing, 3-8 weeks

**Process**:
1. List all avoided financial tasks (0-100 anxiety rating)
2. Order from least to most anxiety-provoking
3. Start with lowest item
4. Practice repeatedly until anxiety reduces by 50%
5. Move to next item

**Example Hierarchy**:
- 20: Check savings account balance
- 30: Open bank statement
- 40: Review credit card charges
- 50: Calculate total debt
- 60: Call credit card company
- 70: Create budget
- 80: Meet with financial adviser
- 90: File overdue tax return
- 100: Declare bankruptcy consultation

**Practice Tips**:
- Stay in situation until anxiety reduces
- Practice regularly (3-5 times per week)
- Don't move up hierarchy too quickly
- Celebrate each step

### 1.3 Behavioral Activation for Financial Tasks

**Learning Styles**: Kinesthetic, Reading/Writing
**Duration**: Weekly planning, daily execution

**Activity Scheduling**:
Create weekly plan linking activities to mood/accomplishment

| Day | Time | Financial Activity | Difficulty (1-10) | Pleasure (After) | Accomplishment (After) |
|-----|------|-------------------|-------------------|------------------|----------------------|
| Mon | 7pm | Review spending for week | 4 | 3 | 7 |
| Wed | 8am | Transfer $50 to savings | 2 | 6 | 8 |
| Fri | 6pm | Update budget spreadsheet | 5 | 4 | 8 |

**Key Principle**: Action comes first, motivation follows
- Schedule activities even when not motivated
- Rate pleasure and accomplishment AFTER completing
- Notice patterns: which activities boost mood most?

### 1.4 Money Scripts Identification

**Learning Styles**: Reading/Writing, Auditory (discussion)
**Duration**: 45-60 minutes

**Four Categories Exercise**:

**Money Avoidance Scripts**:
- "Money is bad/corrupting"
- "Rich people are greedy"
- "I don't deserve money"
- "More money, more problems"

**Money Worship Scripts**:
- "More money will solve all my problems"
- "You can never have enough money"
- "Money brings happiness"
- "Money equals success"

**Money Status Scripts**:
- "Your self-worth equals your net worth"
- "I won't buy anything unless it's new/prestigious"
- "If people knew my real financial situation, they'd think less of me"
- "Poor people are lazy"

**Money Vigilance Scripts** (can be healthy or extreme):
- "You should always save for a rainy day"
- "It's not okay to tell others how much money you have"
- "You shouldn't buy things you can't pay cash for"

**Activity**:
1. Read each statement
2. Rate agreement 1-5
3. Identify where belief came from (family, culture, experience)
4. Evaluate: Does this serve me now?
5. Rewrite if needed

### 1.5 Socratic Questioning for Money Beliefs

**Learning Styles**: Auditory, Reading/Writing
**Duration**: 20 minutes per belief

**Question Sequence**:
For belief: "I'm terrible with money"

1. **Evidence**: "What evidence do you have that you're terrible with money?"
2. **Counter-evidence**: "What evidence suggests you're NOT terrible with money?"
3. **Alternative**: "What's another way to look at this?"
4. **Worst-case**: "If this were true, what would that mean? Could you handle it?"
5. **Best-case**: "What's the best that could happen?"
6. **Most realistic**: "What's the most realistic outcome?"
7. **Friend test**: "If a friend had this belief, what would you tell them?"
8. **Functional**: "Does believing this help you or hurt you?"
9. **Action**: "If you didn't have this belief, what would you do differently?"

## SECTION 2: ACT TECHNIQUES FOR FINANCIAL BEHAVIOR

### 2.1 Values Clarification - Money Edition

**Learning Styles**: Visual, Kinesthetic, Reading/Writing
**Duration**: 60 minutes

**Values Cards Sort**:
Materials: Cards with values written on them

**Values List**:
- Financial Security
- Freedom/Flexibility
- Family/Relationships
- Contribution/Generosity
- Adventure/Experiences
- Achievement
- Health/Wellbeing
- Learning/Growth
- Creativity
- Legacy
- Independence
- Pleasure/Enjoyment

**Activity**:
1. Sort into three piles: Very Important, Somewhat Important, Not Important
2. From "Very Important," choose top 5
3. Rank your top 5 in order
4. For each, write:
   - What this value means to me
   - How I'm currently living this (or not)
   - What money behaviors align with this value
   - What money behaviors conflict with this value

**Values Bull's-Eye**:
Draw four quadrants: Work/Education, Leisure, Relationships, Personal Growth/Health

For each quadrant:
- Mark X where you are now (0-10 scale)
- Mark O where you want to be
- Identify financial behaviors that would move you toward O

### 2.2 Cognitive Defusion Exercises

**Learning Styles**: Auditory, Kinesthetic
**Duration**: 5-10 minutes each

**Technique 1: "I'm having the thought that..."**
- Original: "I'm bad with money"
- Defused: "I'm having the thought that I'm bad with money"
- More defused: "I notice my mind telling me I'm bad with money"

Practice saying out loud 10 times, notice how it changes.

**Technique 2: Silly Voices**
Take your financial worry and:
- Sing it as an opera
- Say it in a cartoon character voice
- Whisper it very slowly
- Say it very quickly

**Technique 3: Leaves on a Stream**
Visualization (5 minutes):
- Picture yourself sitting by a stream
- Each thought is a leaf floating by
- Place money thoughts on leaves
- Watch them float away
- Don't grab them, just observe

**Technique 4: Thank Your Mind**
When financial anxiety thought appears:
- "Thank you mind for trying to protect me"
- "Thanks for the warning, mind, I've got this"

### 2.3 Willingness and Acceptance Practice

**Learning Styles**: Experiential, Auditory
**Duration**: 15 minutes

**Money Discomfort Exposure**:
1. Bring to mind mildly uncomfortable financial situation
2. Notice where you feel it in your body
3. Instead of pushing away, get curious:
   - What's the texture of this feeling?
   - What color would it be?
   - If it had a temperature, what would it be?
   - If it had a shape, what shape?
4. Breathe into the sensation
5. Practice making room for it
6. Say: "I'm willing to feel this to move toward my values"

**Expansion Exercise**:
Bring up financial anxiety, then:
1. Observe it (don't judge)
2. Breathe into it
3. Create space for it
4. Allow it to be there
5. Repeat: "I can feel anxious AND still pay this bill"

### 2.4 Committed Action Planning

**Learning Styles**: Reading/Writing, Visual
**Duration**: 30-45 minutes

**SMART Goals Aligned with Values**:

Template:
- **Value**: What matters most?
- **Goal**: What specific action aligns with this value?
- **Specific**: Exactly what will I do?
- **Measurable**: How will I know I did it?
- **Achievable**: Is this realistic right now?
- **Relevant**: Does this serve my value?
- **Time-bound**: By when?

**Example**:
- Value: Financial Security
- Goal: Build emergency fund
- Specific: Transfer $100 to savings account every payday
- Measurable: Can check account balance
- Achievable: Yes, can cut $100 from discretionary spending
- Relevant: Directly supports financial security
- Time-bound: Start this Friday, continue for 6 months

**Barriers and Willingness**:
- What uncomfortable thoughts/feelings might show up?
- Am I willing to have those and do it anyway?
- What's my committed action plan when barriers arise?

### 2.5 Passengers on the Bus Metaphor

**Learning Styles**: Visual, Kinesthetic, Auditory
**Duration**: 20 minutes

**Exercise**:
You are the bus driver. Your values determine where the bus is going. Various passengers (thoughts, feelings, memories) sit on the bus:

**Financial Anxiety Passengers**:
- The Worrier: "What if we run out of money?"
- The Critic: "You're so irresponsible!"
- The Avoider: "Don't look at the bank account!"
- The Catastrophizer: "We'll end up homeless!"

**Activity**:
1. Draw a bus
2. Draw/label your financial anxiety passengers
3. Notice: They're just passengers, not the driver
4. You can hear them and still drive where YOU choose
5. Practice: "I hear you, Worrier, AND I'm still creating this budget"

**Group Version**: Participants role-play different passengers while one person practices driving the bus toward their values.

## SECTION 3: IFS TECHNIQUES FOR MONEY PARTS

### 3.1 Parts Mapping Exercise

**Learning Styles**: Visual, Kinesthetic
**Duration**: 45-60 minutes

**Materials**: Large paper, colored markers

**Process**:
1. **Center**: Draw yourself (the Self)
2. **Around the center**: Draw/write your money parts
3. **For each part**, note:
   - Name/role (The Spender, The Saver, The Worrier, etc.)
   - What it does (behaviors)
   - What it feels (emotions)
   - What it believes (thoughts)
   - What it's trying to protect you from
   - Age it seems to be stuck at

**Common Money Parts**:
- The Hoarder (Manager - prevents scarcity)
- The Spender (Firefighter - soothes pain)
- The Worrier (Manager - tries to control)
- The Avoider (Manager - prevents overwhelm)
- The Critic (Manager - prevents failure through harsh judgment)
- The Risk-Taker (can be Manager or Firefighter)
- The Victim (Exile - feels powerless)
- The Deprived Child (Exile - never had enough)

### 3.2 Getting to Know a Part (IFS Process)

**Learning Styles**: Experiential, Auditory, Reading/Writing
**Duration**: 30-45 minutes

**6 F's of IFS**:

**1. FIND the part**:
- Where do you notice it in/on your body?
- What does it look like?
- How old does it seem?

**2. FOCUS on it**:
- Bring gentle attention to this part
- Get curious about it
- Notice what you notice

**3. FLESH it out**:
- What does this part do? (behaviors)
- What does it feel? (emotions)
- What does it believe? (thoughts)
- What does it want you to know?

**4. How do you FEEL toward it?**:
If judgment, anger, fear:
- Ask judging part to step back
- See if you can find curiosity, compassion, or calm
- These are signs of Self

**5. Find out what the part is afraid would happen if it didn't do its job** (FEARS):
Example responses:
- Spender Part: "You'd feel the pain underneath"
- Worrier Part: "You'd make reckless decisions"
- Avoider Part: "You'd be overwhelmed by how bad it is"

**6. BEFRIEND the part**:
- Appreciate its positive intention
- Thank it for trying to help
- Ask what it needs from you

**Journal Template**:
```
Part name: _______
What I notice: _______
What it does: _______
What it feels: _______
What it's protecting me from: _______
What I want this part to know: _______
What it needs from me: _______
```

### 3.3 Unburdening Ritual

**Learning Styles**: Kinesthetic, Visual, Experiential
**Duration**: 45-60 minutes

**When**: After a part trusts you and is ready to release old beliefs

**Process**:
1. **Ask the part**: "Are you carrying any beliefs or emotions that feel like a burden?"
2. **Identify the burden**: "I'll never have enough" / "Money is dangerous" / "I don't deserve wealth"
3. **Check if ready**: "Are you ready to release this burden?"
4. **Choose method**:
   - Light: Imagine burden dissolving in light
   - Water: Wash it away in a stream
   - Fire: Burn it away safely
   - Earth: Bury it and let it decompose
   - Wind: Blow it away
5. **Release**: Visualize the burden leaving the part
6. **Invite in**: Ask part what positive qualities it wants instead (security, confidence, trust)
7. **Integration**: How does the part feel now? What's its new role?

**Example Script**:
"Deprived Child part, I see you've been carrying the burden that 'there will never be enough.' You've held this since you were 5 years old, when Dad lost his job. Are you ready to release this old belief that no longer serves us? ...Good. Choose how you'd like to let it go... You're choosing light. Notice the warm light surrounding this belief... watch it dissolve... it's leaving your system now... How do you feel? ...Lighter? Good. What would you like instead of this old belief? ...Trust that we can create abundance? Beautiful. Imagine bringing that quality of trust in now... notice how it fills the space where the burden was... What's your new role in our system? ...You're the one who reminds us of our resourcefulness? Perfect."

### 3.4 Parts Dialogue (Empty Chair)

**Learning Styles**: Kinesthetic, Auditory
**Duration**: 30 minutes

**Setup**: Two chairs facing each other

**Process** (for polarized parts - e.g., Spender vs Saver):

1. **Chair 1 - Spender Part**:
   - Sit in this chair
   - Speak AS the Spender
   - "I want to buy things because..."
   - "I'm trying to help by..."
   - "I'm afraid that if I don't spend..."

2. **Switch to Chair 2 - Saver Part**:
   - Sit in this chair
   - Speak AS the Saver
   - "I want to save everything because..."
   - "I'm trying to protect us by..."
   - "I'm afraid that if we spend..."

3. **Continue dialogue**, switching chairs:
   - Let each part fully express
   - Notice: Both are trying to help
   - Both have valid concerns
   - They're just using extreme strategies

4. **Third chair - Self**:
   - From this chair, speak TO both parts
   - Appreciate both
   - Help them see they're on the same team
   - Facilitate compromise

**Questions to ask each part**:
- "What are you trying to accomplish?"
- "What are you afraid of?"
- "What do you need the other part to understand?"
- "What would you need to relax your extreme role?"

### 3.5 Daily Parts Check-In

**Learning Styles**: Reading/Writing, Auditory
**Duration**: 10 minutes daily

**Morning Practice**:
1. Close eyes, take three breaths
2. Check in with your system: "How are my parts today?"
3. Notice which parts are activated
4. Ask: "What do you need from me today?"
5. Reassure any worried parts
6. Set intention to act from Self

**Evening Practice**:
1. Review day: Which parts showed up?
2. Around money situations, who was driving?
3. Any parts that need attention?
4. Appreciate parts that helped
5. Make notes for tomorrow

**Journal Template**:
```
Date: _______
Parts I noticed today: _______
Situations where they showed up: _______
How I responded from Self: _______
Parts that need more attention: _______
Tomorrow's intention: _______
```

## SECTION 4: ATTACHMENT-BASED EXERCISES

### 4.1 Attachment Style Assessment & Reflection

**Learning Styles**: Reading/Writing, Intrapersonal
**Duration**: 30 minutes

**Part A: Identify Your Style**

Read these descriptions and note which resonates most:

**Secure**: I'm comfortable depending on others and having others depend on me. I don't worry about being alone or about others not accepting me.

**Anxious-Preoccupied**: I worry that others won't care about me as much as I care about them. I worry about being abandoned. I need a lot of reassurance.

**Dismissive-Avoidant**: I'm very independent and don't need close relationships. I prefer not to depend on others or have them depend on me. I keep my emotional distance.

**Fearful-Avoidant**: I'm uncomfortable getting too close to others. I want emotionally close relationships but find it difficult to trust or depend on others completely.

**Part B: Money Manifestations**

For your primary style, journal:
1. How does this show up with money?
2. In my relationship with financial institutions?
3. In financial relationships with partners/family?
4. What financial behaviors might be attachment-driven?
5. What would more "secure" financial attachment look like?

### 4.2 Couple Financial Attachment Exercise

**Learning Styles**: Interpersonal, Auditory
**Duration**: 45 minutes

**Requirement**: Both partners willing to participate

**Structure**:
1. **Individual reflection** (10 min each, separately):
   - My attachment style
   - My money messages from childhood
   - My financial fears
   - What I need to feel financially secure

2. **Sharing** (15 min):
   - Partner A shares, Partner B listens (no interrupting)
   - Partner B reflects back what they heard
   - Switch roles

3. **Dialogue** (20 min):
   - How do our attachment styles interact around money?
   - What financial interactions trigger my attachment fears?
   - What could my partner do to help me feel more secure?
   - How can we create a "secure base" for financial discussions?

**Communication Guidelines**:
- Use "I feel" statements
- No criticism or defensiveness
- Curiosity, not judgment
- Validate each other's experiences
- Remember: You're on the same team

### 4.3 Secure Base Visualization

**Learning Styles**: Auditory, Visual, Intrapersonal
**Duration**: 15 minutes

**Guided Practice** (can be recorded):

"Close your eyes and take three deep breaths... Imagine a place where you feel completely safe and supported... This is your secure financial base... It might be a physical place, a person, or just a feeling... Notice what makes this place feel secure... What does it look like? ... What's the quality of the space?... How does your body feel here?... 

From this secure base, imagine approaching a financial challenge... Notice that you can take risks because you know you have this safe place to return to... You can explore, make mistakes, learn... and always come back to safety... 

What would change in your financial life if you always had access to this secure base?... What risks might you take?... What conversations might you have?... 

Take a deep breath... and when you're ready, open your eyes... Journal about your experience."

**Reflection Questions**:
- What represents a secure base for me financially?
- How can I create more of this feeling?
- Who in my life provides this security?
- How can I be a secure base for myself around money?

### 4.4 Protest Behavior Recognition

**Learning Styles**: Reading/Writing, Intrapersonal
**Duration**: 20 minutes

**Concept**: Protest behaviors are attempts to get attachment figure's attention when feeling insecure.

**Financial Protest Behaviors**:

Anxious Attachment:
- Excessive spending to get partner's attention
- Creating financial "emergencies" for reassurance
- Checking accounts obsessively
- Demanding constant financial updates from partner

Avoidant Attachment:
- Financial independence to extreme (refusing help)
- Making unilateral financial decisions
- Withdrawing from money discussions
- Hiding financial information

**Exercise**:
1. Review last 3 months of financial behaviors
2. Identify any that might be "protests"
3. What unmet attachment need was underneath?
4. What's a more direct way to get that need met?

Example:
- Behavior: Impulse-bought expensive item
- Protest against: Feeling financially controlled by partner
- Underlying need: Autonomy and respect
- Direct approach: "I need us to discuss how we make financial decisions together. I'm feeling like I don't have a voice."

### 4.5 Earned Security Practice

**Learning Styles**: Reading/Writing, Experiential
**Duration**: Ongoing, 30-day practice

**Concept**: You can develop more secure attachment through conscious practice, even if you didn't have it in childhood.

**Daily Practices**:

**Week 1-2: Self-Regulation**
- Notice when you're financially activated (anxious/avoidant)
- Pause before reacting
- Take 3 deep breaths
- Choose a response from your adult self, not your attachment wounds
- Journal about the experience

**Week 3-4: Reaching Out**
- Practice appropriate financial interdependence
- Ask for help once this week
- Offer help once this week
- Share a financial vulnerability with a trusted person
- Notice: reaching out doesn't mean disaster

**Week 5-6: Trust Building**
- Take small financial risks
- Share financial goals with someone supportive
- Follow through on financial commitments
- Notice you can be trusted and trust others
- Celebrate wins, no matter how small

**Tracking Template**:
```
Date: _______
Situation: _______
My attachment reaction: _______
What I chose to do instead: _______
Result: _______
What I learned: _______
```

## SECTION 5: SCHEMA THERAPY EXERCISES

### 5.1 Schema Questionnaire Self-Assessment

**Learning Styles**: Reading/Writing
**Duration**: 45 minutes

**Young Schema Questionnaire - Selected Items**

Rate each 1-6 (1=Completely untrue, 6=Describes me perfectly):

**Abandonment/Instability**:
___ I worry that people I'm close to will leave me or abandon me
___ I feel that I don't have anyone to depend on financially

**Defectiveness/Shame**:
___ I'm fundamentally flawed and don't deserve financial success
___ If people knew the real me financially, they wouldn't want to be with me

**Failure**:
___ I'm incompetent when it comes to financial matters
___ Most other people are better at finances than I am

**Vulnerability to Harm**:
___ I worry that I'm developing a serious financial problem
___ I feel that a financial disaster could strike at any moment

**Emotional Deprivation**:
___ No one really understands my financial situation
___ I have not had one person who can give me financial support and guidance

**Self-Sacrifice**:
___ I put others' financial needs before my own
___ I feel guilty if I spend money on myself

**Unrelenting Standards**:
___ I must be perfect in my financial management
___ If I don't meet the highest financial standards, I'm a failure

**After assessment**:
1. Identify 2-3 strongest schemas
2. For each, trace back: When did this belief form?
3. What evidence was there that made sense then?
4. Does this belief serve you now?
5. What's the cost of maintaining this schema?

### 5.2 Schema Flashcard Exercise

**Learning Styles**: Visual, Reading/Writing
**Duration**: 15 minutes initial creation, 5 minutes daily review

**Create flashcards** (physical or digital):

**Front of Card**:
Schema-based belief (e.g., "I don't deserve financial success - Defectiveness Schema")

**Back of Card**:
Rational response:
- "This is my defectiveness schema speaking
- It formed when [childhood event]
- Evidence it's not true: [list 3+ items]
- What I know now: I'm learning and growing, deserving isn't relevant - everyone can learn financial skills
- Action to take: [specific behavior]"

**Daily Practice**:
- Review cards each morning
- When schema activates during day, pull out relevant card
- Read rational response
- Practice opposite action (schema-busting behavior)

### 5.3 Imagery Rescripting for Money Memories

**Learning Styles**: Auditory, Visual, Experiential
**Duration**: 45-60 minutes (facilitated)

**Warning**: This is deep work - consider doing with a trained therapist first

**Process**:

1. **Identify early memory** where schema formed:
   - "I remember when I was [age]..."
   - Financial event that was shaming, frightening, depriving, etc.

2. **Enter the memory** as if watching a movie:
   - See your child self
   - Notice what's happening
   - What's the child feeling?
   - What does the child need?

3. **Adult self enters the scene**:
   - Current-age you steps into the memory
   - Approach the child
   - Provide what was missing:
     - Comfort the child
     - Protect from harm
     - Give guidance
     - Stand up to perpetrators if needed
     - Meet the unmet need

4. **Rescript the ending**:
   - What would healthy adult do/say?
   - What does child need to hear?
   - How does scene end differently?
   - How does child feel now?

5. **Integration**:
   - Open eyes
   - Journal about experience
   - What changed?
   - What does that part of you need now?

**Example**:
Original: "I was 7, Dad screaming about money, saying we might lose the house, I felt terrified and helpless"

Rescripted: "I see 7-year-old me scared in the corner. Adult me goes to her, sits beside her, says 'You're safe. This isn't your responsibility. You're just a child. I'm here now and I can handle this. Dad is overwhelmed but that's not about you. You're going to be okay.' I take her hand and we leave the room together. She feels safe now."

### 5.4 Schema Mode Mapping

**Learning Styles**: Visual, Kinesthetic
**Duration**: 60 minutes

**Materials**: Large paper, colored markers

**Draw a circle divided into sections** for different modes:

**Vulnerable Child Mode** (scared, sad, hurt):
- When activated financially: _______
- Feelings: _______
- Needs: _______

**Angry Child Mode** (furious, defiant):
- When activated: _______
- What I'm angry about: _______

**Impulsive Child Mode** (reckless, "I want it now!"):
- When activated: _______
- Behaviors: _______

**Happy Child Mode** (playful, content):
- When experienced financially: _______
- What brings this out: _______

**Demanding Parent Mode** (harsh, critical):
- What it says about money: _______
- Standards it holds: _______

**Healthy Adult Mode** (balanced, wise):
- How it approaches money: _______
- What it sounds like: _______

**Activity**:
1. Map out your modes
2. Track for one week: Which mode was active when?
3. Practice: How can Healthy Adult respond to other modes?

### 5.5 Pattern-Breaking Behavioral Experiments

**Learning Styles**: Kinesthetic, Experiential
**Duration**: Ongoing, weekly experiments

**Process**:
1. Identify schema-driven pattern
2. Design experiment to break pattern
3. Predict what you fear will happen
4. Do the experiment
5. Record what actually happened
6. Update schema

**Example Experiments**:

**For Defectiveness Schema**:
- Pattern: Hide financial mistakes
- Experiment: Tell trusted friend about recent financial mistake
- Feared outcome: They'll judge me, reject me
- Actual outcome: They shared their own mistakes, felt closer
- Learning: Vulnerability creates connection, not rejection

**For Failure Schema**:
- Pattern: Avoid trying new financial skills
- Experiment: Take one small financial risk (try new budgeting app)
- Feared outcome: I'll mess it up and feel like a failure
- Actual outcome: Learned something new, felt accomplished
- Learning: I can learn new things, mistakes are part of growth

**For Unrelenting Standards**:
- Pattern: Perfect budget or no budget
- Experiment: Make "good enough" budget with realistic numbers
- Feared outcome: It won't work, I'll lose control
- Actual outcome: Good enough worked better than perfect
- Learning: Progress over perfection

**Template**:
```
Schema: _______
Current pattern: _______
Experiment I'll try: _______
What I predict will happen: _______
What actually happened: _______
What I learned: _______
Schema rating before (1-10): ___
Schema rating after (1-10): ___
```

## SECTION 6: INTEGRATIVE EXERCISES

### 6.1 Money Autobiography

**Learning Styles**: Reading/Writing, Intrapersonal, Auditory (if shared)
**Duration**: 2-3 hours total

**Instructions**: Write your money story in chronological chapters

**Chapter 1: Early Childhood (0-7)**
- First money memory
- Parents' relationship with money
- Messages received about money
- Family financial situation
- Formative experiences

**Chapter 2: Middle Childhood (8-12)**
- Money and peer relationships
- Allowance, chores, earning
- Spending vs saving lessons
- Comparisons to others
- Significant money events

**Chapter 3: Adolescence (13-18)**
- Part-time jobs
- Financial independence
- Money and identity
- Conflicts about money
- Preparation for financial adulthood

**Chapter 4: Young Adulthood (19-30)**
- First major financial decisions
- Student loans, credit cards
- Financial mistakes and successes
- Relationship money conflicts
- Schema activation points

**Chapter 5: Adulthood (30+)**
- Current patterns
- Themes that persist
- What you've changed
- What still needs work
- Where you want to go

**Integration Questions**:
1. What themes run through my money story?
2. Which schemas do I see developing?
3. What attachment patterns emerged?
4. Which money scripts did I inherit?
5. What parts of me were trying to adapt to circumstances?
6. What do I want the next chapter to include?

### 6.2 Cross-Modal Money Tree

**Learning Styles**: Visual, Kinesthetic, Intrapersonal
**Duration**: 60-90 minutes

**Materials**: Large paper, colored markers

**Draw a tree representing your financial psychology**:

**Roots** (Origins - Attachment & Schema):
- Childhood experiences with money
- Family financial patterns
- Early wounds/trauma
- Core unmet needs
- Formative messages

**Trunk** (Core Beliefs - Schemas):
- Early maladaptive schemas about money
- Money scripts
- Attachment style around finances

**Branches** (Parts - IFS):
- Different money parts
- Their roles and strategies
- Protective mechanisms
- Young wounded parts

**Leaves** (Thoughts - CBT):
- Automatic thoughts about money
- Cognitive distortions
- Self-talk

**Fruit** (Values & Actions - ACT):
- True values
- Aligned behaviors
- Areas of growth
- Aspirational fruits

**Weather** (Environment):
- Current financial stressors
- Support system
- Resources available

**Process**:
1. Draw and label all components
2. Notice connections: How do roots feed trunk feed branches?
3. Identify: What needs healing at each level?
4. Plan: What intervention at which level?

## SECTION 7: GROUP FACILITATION EXERCISES

### 7.1 Money Shame Circle

**Learning Styles**: Interpersonal, Auditory
**Duration**: 60 minutes
**Group Size**: 6-12 people

**Process**:
1. **Round 1 - Ice Breaker** (10 min):
   - Share first money memory (nothing too vulnerable)

2. **Round 2 - Shame Sharing** (20 min):
   - "One financial thing I'm ashamed of is..."
   - No cross-talk, just listening
   - Each person shares 2-3 minutes

3. **Round 3 - Witnessing** (15 min):
   - "What I noticed about our shared humanity..."
   - Themes that emerged
   - Universal struggles

4. **Round 4 - Compassion** (15 min):
   - "Something I want you to know about your financial struggle..."
   - Offering compassion to person on your right
   - Receiving compassion from person on your left

**Debrief**:
- How did it feel to share?
- What surprised you?
- How does shame shift when shared?

### 7.2 Parts Theater

**Learning Styles**: Kinesthetic, Interpersonal, Auditory
**Duration**: 45-60 minutes
**Group Size**: 4-8 people

**Activity**:
1. Volunteer shares a financial conflict (internal or external)
2. Group members role-play their parts:
   - One person: The Spender
   - One person: The Saver
   - One person: The Critic
   - One person: The Avoider
   - One person: Self (facilitator)

3. Parts interact while volunteer observes
4. Volunteer can direct: "Spender, what are you feeling?"
5. Self character helps parts understand each other
6. Group processes: What did you notice? What resonated?

**Variation**: Rotating roles - everyone gets to play each part

### 7.3 Values Marketplace

**Learning Styles**: Kinesthetic, Interpersonal, Visual
**Duration**: 45 minutes
**Group Size**: 8-20 people

**Setup**:
- Large room with stations around perimeter
- Each station represents a value (Family, Security, Adventure, etc.)
- Participants given 10 poker chips (representing resources)

**Activity**:
1. **Round 1** - Current State:
   - Distribute your chips to stations based on where your money/time/energy actually goes now
   - Stand back and observe the marketplace
   - Notice misalignments

2. **Round 2** - Ideal State:
   - Collect your chips
   - Redistribute based on where you'd LIKE resources to go
   - Notice differences

3. **Small Groups** (3-4 people):
   - Share observations
   - What needs to change?
   - What barriers exist?
   - One committed action

### 7.4 Financial Genogram Exercise

**Learning Styles**: Visual, Interpersonal, Reading/Writing
**Duration**: 90 minutes
**Group Size**: Any

**Materials**: Large paper, markers

**Individual Work** (30 min):
Draw family tree 3 generations back, noting:
- Financial patterns (earner, saver, spender, avoider, hoarder, gambler)
- Money scripts transmitted
- Financial traumas
- Wealth/poverty
- Gender messages about money
- Cultural influences

**Partner Sharing** (30 min):
- Share genogram with partner
- What patterns do you see?
- What did you inherit?
- What do you want to change?

**Large Group** (30 min):
- Volunteers share insights
- Common themes
- Cultural variations
- Breaking generational patterns

### 7.5 ACT Money Choice Point Exercise

**Learning Styles**: Visual, Kinesthetic, Interpersonal
**Duration**: 30 minutes
**Group Size**: Any

**Setup**:
Draw large choice point diagram on floor with tape:
- Center point: Present moment
- Arrow up: Toward values (labeled)
- Arrow down: Away from values (labeled)
- Left side: Thoughts & feelings (hooks)
- Right side: Actions

**Activity**:
1. Volunteers stand at center point
2. Describe financial situation
3. Notice thoughts/feelings (step left)
4. Step back to center
5. Clarify value (look up the toward arrow)
6. Name action that moves toward value
7. Step onto toward arrow
8. Take action (even with uncomfortable feelings)

**Group Process**:
- What did you notice?
- How does it feel to choose values with discomfort present?
- Practice scenarios
