# Your Money Persona Quiz
## Discover Your Unique Financial Psychology Profile

### Introduction

Everyone has a unique relationship with money shaped by their childhood, relationships, and experiences. This quiz will help you understand YOUR specific patterns so you can work with them, not against them.

**What you'll discover**:
- Your primary Money Persona
- Why you do what you do with money
- Your specific challenges and strengths
- A personalized action plan

**Time**: 10-15 minutes

---

## PERSONA 1: THE BALANCED BUILDER

### Your Profile

**Core Pattern**: You have a relatively healthy relationship with money. You can plan, save, and spend without excessive anxiety or avoidance. You see money as a tool.

**Strengths**:
- Can make decisions without excessive stress
- Balance present enjoyment with future planning
- Open to learning and adjusting
- Can handle setbacks without spiraling
- Generally comfortable discussing money

**Challenges**:
- Might take your financial health for granted
- Can become complacent about planning
- May not always recognize emotional aspects of money
- Could be more intentional about values alignment

**Where This Comes From**:
- Generally secure childhood relationships
- Financial stability in upbringing (or learned healthy patterns despite instability)

- Able to trust yourself and others

**Your Relationship Style**: Secure & Comfortable

- You handle financial intimacy well
- You're not threatened by partner's different approach

**Your Primary Parts**:

**Your Typical Thoughts**:

**4. Support Others**

- Create safe space for money conversations
- Be patient with others' patterns
- Use Exercise: Couples Money History (Workbook pg. __)

**5. Optimize the Edges**
- You have bandwidth others don't
- Focus on optimization (tax strategy, investments)
- Learn something new financially each quarter
- Give back/teach others

## PERSONA 2: THE ANXIOUS PROTECTOR

### Your Profile

**Core Pattern**: You worry about money constantly, even when objectively okay. You're hyper-vigilant about financial security, scan for threats, and never feel like you have "enough."

**Strengths**:
- Excellent at spotting financial risks
- Motivated to save and build security
- Attentive to financial details
- Protective of loved ones
- Unlikely to make reckless decisions

**Challenges**:
- Constant financial anxiety regardless of reality
- Difficulty enjoying what you have
- Check accounts obsessively
- Hard to spend even on legitimate needs
- Might overwork to feel secure
- Need lots of reassurance from partner
- Catastrophize financial situations

**Where This Comes From**:
- Financial instability in childhood
- Parents who worried constantly about money
- Experience of loss or scarcity
- Learned that the world isn't safe

**Your Relationship Style**: Anxious & Worried
- You fear abandonment (including financial abandonment)
- Need reassurance from partner about finances
- Might cling or check in constantly about money
- Worry partner will leave if money gets tight

**Your Primary Parts**:
- **The Worrier** (overdrive mode) - constantly scanning for danger
- **The Saver/Hoarder** - protects by keeping everything
- **The Scared Kid** - remembers childhood financial insecurity

**Your Typical Thoughts**:
- "What if we run out?"
- "Is this enough?"
- "Something bad is going to happen"
- "We should save more, just in case"

---

### YOUR ACTION PLAN: The Anxious Protector

**Your Focus**: Calming the worry while maintaining security

**Don't Try To**:
- Eliminate all anxiety (won't work)
- Force yourself to "just relax" about money
- Shame yourself for worrying
- Spend recklessly to "prove" you're not anxious

**DO Focus On**:

**1. Reality-Testing Your Fears (Weekly)**
Write down:
- What I'm worried about: ___________
- Likelihood 1-10: ___
- Evidence it's actually happening: ___________
- What I can control: ___________

**Use Exercise**: Thought Log (Workbook pg. __)

**2. Create Concrete Security**
Anxiety needs something tangible:
- Build emergency fund (specific target)
- Automatic savings so you're "always" saving
- Written financial plan
- Insurance for specific fears
- Career backup plan

These give Worrier part something real to point to.

**3. Scheduled Worry Time**
- Set 15 min daily for financial worry
- Write all worries in a notebook
- Outside that time, tell yourself "I'll worry about that at 7pm"
- Contain the anxiety instead of letting it run all day

**4. Limit Account Checking**
- Once a day maximum (or whatever is less than now)
- Set specific times
- Use app notifications only for real issues
- Notice: Checking doesn't actually keep you safer

**Use Exercise**: Daily Parts Check-In (Workbook pg. __)

**5. Talk to Your Worrier Part**
This part is trying to protect you.

**Dialogue**:
You: "Worrier, I see you're scared about money"
Worrier: "Of course! Remember when [childhood event]?"
You: "I know that was terrifying. But I'm an adult now with resources. Here's what we have..." [list actual security]
Worrier: "But what if..."
You: "I hear you. I've got this. Let me show you the plan."

**Use Exercise**: Getting to Know a Part (Workbook pg. __)

**6. Practice "And" Statements**
- "I feel anxious about money AND I'm actually okay right now"
- "I'm worried about the future AND I can handle what comes"
- "I feel scared AND the evidence says I'm secure"

**7. Build Earned Security**
Practice gradually trusting:
- Take one small financial risk
- Spend on one need without guilt
- Let partner handle one money task
- Notice: You're okay even with less control

**Use Exercise**: Building Security Practice (Workbook pg. __)

---

### Your Daily Practice (10 min)

**Morning**:
- Reality check: "Am I in actual danger today? No."
- One concrete thing I have (emergency fund, income, skills)
- Intention: "I can feel worried and still be okay"

**Evening**:
- What did I worry about today?
- Did those things happen?
- What can I let go of?

---

### Your Weekly Practice (30 min)

**Structure calms anxiety**:
- Review actual financial numbers (facts, not fears)
- One worry to reality-test
- One thing that went well
- Talk to Worrier part: "What do you need this week?"

---

### Your Mantras

- "I can feel anxious and still be okay"
- "I have enough for today"
- "My worry is trying to protect me, but I'm safe"
- "The feeling will pass, the facts remain"

---

### For Partners of Anxious Protectors

**If this is your partner**:
- They're not trying to be controlling—they're scared
- Give reassurance without enabling constant checking
- Weekly money meeting helps (predictable reassurance)
- Don't dismiss their fears, help reality-test them
- Celebrate when they take small risks

**Use Exercise**: Couples Money History (Workbook pg. __)

---

### When You're Really Stuck

**Red flags you need extra help**:
- Anxiety prevents you from working or functioning
- Can't sleep due to money worry
- Obsessive account checking (10+ times/day)
- Can't spend on basic needs despite having money
- Suicidal thoughts about finances

**Get support**: Therapist who understands anxiety + financial planner for concrete security

---

### Your Growth Edge

**You're growing when**:
- You notice worry without it taking over
- You can spend on legitimate needs
- You check accounts less frequently
- You take small financial risks
- You trust yourself (and partner) more
- You enjoy what you have sometimes

**Remember**: You don't have to become reckless. You're learning to trust yourself while staying responsible.

---

### Resources for Anxious Protectors

- **Books**: "The Anxiety and Worry Workbook" (CBT approach), "The Art of Money" (emotional work)
- **Apps**: Insight Timer (meditations for anxiety)
- **Support**: Therapist for anxiety + financial planner for concrete plans

## PERSONA 3: THE AVOIDANT FREE SPIRIT

### Your Profile

**Core Pattern**: You avoid dealing with money. You don't open bills, check accounts, or think about the future. Money feels restrictive and overwhelming. You value independence highly.

**Strengths**:
- Live in the present
- Not controlled by financial anxiety
- Value experiences over things
- Independent spirit
- Can take risks others can't
- Don't let money run your life

**Challenges**:
- Avoid looking at bank accounts
- Don't open bills or mail
- Procrastinate on financial tasks
- Make decisions impulsively to avoid discomfort
- Problems grow because you don't address them early
- Resist help or advice
- If partnered: withdraw from money conversations

**Where This Comes From**:
- Money was overwhelming or shameful in childhood
- Used avoidance to cope with financial stress
- Learned to be very self-reliant
- Fear of being controlled (financially or otherwise)
- Negative associations with money management

**Your Relationship Style**: Avoidant & Independent
- You keep emotional (and financial) distance
- Don't like needing others
- Resist financial interdependence
- If partnered: want separate accounts, avoid joint planning

**Your Primary Parts**:
- **The Avoider** (in charge) - "if I don't look, it's not real"
- **The Free Spirit** - "don't fence me in"
- **The Scared Kid** - overwhelmed by money stuff

**Your Typical Thoughts**:
- "I'll deal with it later"
- "I don't want to think about this"
- "Budgets are restrictive"
- "I should be able to handle this alone"

---

### YOUR ACTION PLAN: The Avoidant Free Spirit

**Your Focus**: Tiny steps and making peace with looking

**Don't Try To**:
- Become a detailed budgeter overnight
- Force yourself to "face everything" at once
- Shame yourself for avoiding
- Let anyone pressure you into their system

**DO Focus On**:

**1. Tiny Steps Approach**
The key: SO SMALL you can't say no

**Week 1**: Just log in to bank account (don't look at balance)
**Week 2**: Look at balance (don't look at transactions)
**Week 3**: Look at one week of transactions
**Week 4**: List all accounts you have
**Week 5**: Check one bill

**Use Exercise**: Tiny Steps for Financial Avoidance (Workbook pg. __)

**2. Talk to Your Avoider Part**
It's protecting you from overwhelm.

**Dialogue**:
You: "Avoider, I know you're trying to protect me"
Avoider: "Yes! Looking at money is overwhelming and scary"
You: "I get it. What if we just look at one tiny thing?"
Avoider: "...okay, but just one thing"
You: "Deal. Thank you for trying to help me."

**Use Exercise**: Getting to Know a Part (Workbook pg. __)

**3. Practice the "And" Technique**
- "I feel scared looking at this AND I can do it"
- "I feel overwhelmed AND I can take one small step"
- "I want to avoid this AND I'm stronger than I think"

**4. Make It Less Overwhelming**

**Reduce stimulation**:
- Turn off notifications (they create anxiety)
- Designate one place for all financial stuff
- Open mail immediately (sitting by recycling)
- Unsubscribe from financial emails you don't need

**Get support**:
- Friend who sits with you while you look
- Financial organizer who helps sort the mess
- Therapist who understands avoidance

**5. Build Accountability Without Shame**

Find someone who:
- Won't judge you
- Will check in gently
- Celebrates small steps
- Doesn't take over (you do the task, they just witness)

**NOT someone who**:
- Makes you feel bad
- Takes control
- Tells you what to do

**6. Challenge "I Should Handle This Alone"**

You wouldn't learn guitar without lessons.
You wouldn't climb a mountain without a guide.
Money is a skill. Asking for help is smart, not weak.

**Use Exercise**: Building Security Practice (Workbook pg. __)

**7. Create Financial "Good Enough"**

Perfectionism feeds avoidance.
You avoid because you think it has to be perfect.

**Try instead**:
- Good enough budget (not perfect)
- Rough sense of spending (not every penny)
- General plan (not detailed analysis)
- Progress over perfection

## PERSONA 4: THE WOUNDED WARRIOR

### Your Profile

**Core Pattern**: You have deep shame and pain about money. You feel fundamentally broken or undeserving. You might self-sabotage success or swing between extremes (saving everything, then blowing it all).

**Strengths**:
- Deep self-awareness (you KNOW something is off)
- Resilience - you keep trying despite pain
- Empathy for others' financial struggles
- Motivation to heal and change
- When you do well, you appreciate it

**Challenges**:
- Intense shame about money
- Self-sabotage when things go well
- Inconsistent with money (save then blow it)
- Feel you don't deserve financial success
- Either chase money to prove worth OR avoid it completely
- Relationship conflicts centered on money
- Difficulty trusting yourself with financial decisions

**Where This Comes From**:
- Childhood poverty or financial instability
- Shame messages about money
- Family conflicts about money
- Feeling "less than" peers
- Trauma around money

**Your Relationship Style**: Fearful & Conflicted
- Want security but sabotage it
- Push people away then pull them close
- Money feels chaotic in relationships
- Trust issues with self AND others

**Your Primary Parts**:
- **The Ashamed One** (exile) - carries deep wound
- **The Self-Saboteur** (protector) - keeps you from scary success
- **The Scared Kid** (exile) - remembers pain
- **The Inner Critic** (protector) - harsh judgments

**Your Typical Thoughts**:
- "I don't deserve this"
- "I'm fundamentally broken with money"
- "I'll never get ahead"
- "See? I always mess it up"

---

### YOUR ACTION PLAN: The Wounded Warrior

**Your Focus**: Healing the deep wound + building self-trust

**Don't Try To**:
- "Just get over it"
- Fix everything at once
- Shame yourself for having wounds
- Bypass the pain to jump to solutions

**DO Focus On**:

**1. FIRST: Get Professional Support**

This is deep work. Consider:
- Therapist who understands trauma
- Financial therapist (both psychology + money)
- Support group

You've likely been trying to do this alone for years. It's time for help.

**2. Identify Your Core Wound**

Read these, which resonates?
- "I don't deserve money" (defectiveness)
- "Money will be taken from me" (abandonment)
- "I can't handle money" (failure)
- "I must take care of everyone else first" (self-sacrifice)

**Use Exercise**: Identifying Your Core Money Wound (Workbook pg. __)

**3. Trace It Back**

Your wound makes sense given your story.

**When did this belief form?**
- Age: _____
- What happened: ___________
- What I concluded: ___________

**Healing**: That belief helped you survive then. It hurts you now.

**Use Exercise**: Healing the Younger You (Workbook pg. __)

**4. Work with Self-Sabotage Pattern**

**Notice when it happens**:
- You get close to success, then... (blow money, quit job, pick fight)
- Right before what? (promotion, windfall, security)

**Ask**: What part is scared of success?

Often the answer:
- "People will expect too much"
- "I don't know how to be successful"
- "Success isn't safe for people like me"
- "I'll become someone I don't like"

**Challenge**: "I can be successful and still be me"

**Use Exercise**: Pattern Recognition for Self-Sabotage (Workbook pg. __)

**5. Create Belief Cards**

**Front**: Old wound belief
**Back**: New truth + evidence

**Example**:
Front: "I don't deserve money"
Back: "Deserving isn't relevant. I'm capable. Evidence: I learned X, accomplished Y, handled Z. I'm learning money skills like I learn anything."

**Read daily**, especially before old pattern activates.

**Use Exercise**: Belief Cards (Workbook pg. __)

**6. Practice Opposite Action**

**Small rebellion against the wound**:
- Wound says: "Don't spend on yourself"
  Action: Buy one small need guilt-free

- Wound says: "You'll fail"
  Action: Take one tiny financial risk and succeed

- Wound says: "Hide your money situation"
  Action: Tell one safe person one truth

**Use Exercise**: Pattern-Breaking Experiments (Workbook pg. __)

**7. Build Self-Trust Gradually**

You can't trust yourself with big things yet.
Start tiny:
- Week 1: Keep one tiny money promise to yourself
- Week 2: Keep another
- Week 3: Keep another
- [Continue building]

Notice: "I'm keeping promises to myself"

**8. Work with Shame**

Shame keeps wounds alive.

**When shame activates**:
- Name it: "This is shame talking"
- Compassion: "This makes sense given my history"
- Truth: "I'm not broken, I'm hurt. Hurt can heal."
- Share: Tell one safe person (shame hates being spoken)

## PERSONA 5: THE PERFECTIONIST ANALYZER

### Your Profile

**Core Pattern**: You need to do money "perfectly." You research exhaustively, track every penny, criticize every decision. You're paralyzed by fear of making the wrong choice.

**Strengths**:
- Excellent researcher
- Detail-oriented
- Thoughtful decision-maker
- Responsible
- Unlikely to make impulsive mistakes
- High standards drive good outcomes

**Challenges**:
- Paralysis by analysis - can't decide
- Track every penny exhaustively
- Harsh self-criticism about money
- All-or-nothing (perfect budget or chaos)
- Research forever, never act
- Beat yourself up over small mistakes
- Impossible standards

**Where This Comes From**:
- Love/approval was conditional growing up
- Nothing you did was good enough
- Praise only for achievement
- Mistakes were harshly criticized
- Learned: "If I'm perfect, I'm safe"

**Your Relationship Style**: Can be Secure or Anxious
- Often secure in relationships
- But: perfectionistic about joint finances
- Partner might feel criticized
- Hard to delegate financial tasks

**Your Primary Parts**:
- **The Perfectionist** (manager) - "It must be perfect"
- **The Inner Critic** (manager) - harsh judgments
- **The Analyzer** (manager) - researches everything
- **The Scared Kid** (exile) - wasn't good enough

**Your Typical Thoughts**:
- "I should be better at this"
- "Did I choose the right option?"
- "I need to research more before deciding"
- "I can't believe I made that mistake"

---

### YOUR ACTION PLAN: The Perfectionist Analyzer

**Your Focus**: Good enough > Perfect

**Don't Try To**:
- Lower your standards to zero
- Stop caring about doing well
- Become reckless
- Never research

**DO Focus On**:

**1. Embrace "Good Enough"**

**The truth**: 
- 80% solution NOW > 100% solution never
- Good enough budget you use > perfect budget you don't
- Imperfect action > perfect paralysis

**Practice**:
- Make decisions with "good enough" information
- Set time limits on research (1 hour, then decide)
- B+ is plenty good enough

**Use Exercise**: Identify Perfectionism Patterns (Workbook pg. __)

**2. Challenge the Inner Critic**

That harsh voice isn't helping you.

**When critic speaks**:
- Notice it: "There's the critic"
- Thank it: "Thanks for trying to keep me safe"
- Redirect: "But harsh criticism doesn't actually help. What's a kinder way to look at this?"

**Replace criticism with coaching**:
Critic: "I'm such an idiot for that mistake"
Coach: "That didn't work. What can I learn?"

**Use Exercise**: Socratic Questions (Workbook pg. __)

**3. Set Boundaries on Research**

**The perfectionist trap**:
- Research forever to make the "perfect" choice
- Miss opportunities while researching
- Overwhelm yourself with information
- Never feel "ready" to decide

**New approach**:
- Set research time limit: "I'll research for 2 hours, then decide"
- Decision deadline: "I'll choose by Friday"
- Good enough criteria: "3 pros outweigh cons? Good enough."

**4. Practice "Done Is Better Than Perfect"**

**Experiments**:
- Week 1: Create "good enough" budget in 30 min (not perfect)
- Week 2: Make financial decision with 70% information
- Week 3: Track spending roughly, not to the penny
- Week 4: Make one tiny mistake on purpose, notice you survive

**Use Exercise**: Pattern-Breaking Experiments (Workbook pg. __)

**5. Work with All-or-Nothing**

**The pattern**:
- Perfect budget or no budget
- Track everything or track nothing
- All savings or all spending

**New approach**:
- What's the middle ground?
- Can I have a B+ budget?
- Can I track most things but not everything?
- Can I be responsible AND flexible?

**6. Challenge "Should" Statements**

Notice how often you say "I should..."
- "I should be better at this"
- "I should have known"
- "I should save more"

**Replace with**:
- "I'm learning"
- "I'm doing reasonably well"
- "I'm making progress"

**7. Celebrate Progress, Not Perfection**

You're so focused on what's wrong, you miss what's right.

**Daily practice**:
- One thing I did adequately with money today
- One place where "good enough" was actually good enough
- One mistake I made that didn't destroy my life
